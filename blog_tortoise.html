Wildlife Conservation IoT Solution for Tortoise Hatchery at DeKUT Wildlife Conservancy

DeKUT Wildlife Conservancy is one of Kenyas Wildlife conservancies in Mount Kenya region in central Kenya. It was previously under indigenous forest cover and home to Kenyan freedom fighters. Currently, 140 acres is under Dedan Kimathi University of Technology as sanctuary for ecosystems and wildlife. It is a home to 9 species of grazing mammals, 2 species of primates, amphibians, many bird species and reptiles such as tortoise.

Problem
This blog describes technology solution solving a challenge of hatching tortoise eggs which take long to hatch and are sensitive to hatching conditions. At DeKUT wildlife conservancy there are 6 adult tortoises. They were brought to the conservancy in the year 2012/2014 from Tsavo, Mara, Amboseli and Nakuru parks. Hitherto, their number has been constant. After acclimating in the conservancy three months later they started laying eggs and buried them as a way of hatching them. Ever since the eggs never hatched. After some research by David, Rashid and Namoni, they found out that the temperature, humidity, eggs safety and Nesting grounds factors inhibited the process.


Figure 1: Locally assembled incubator with tortoise eggs

Introduction
Ecology is changing and some animal species are facing extinction threat. Human activities, natural phenomenon and climate change are the main factors. They affect their life, natural habitats, food chain and reproduction. In order to conserve wildlife, animals species monitoring and tracking, technology aided breeding and wellness are given priority. Wildlife supplement various economies through tourism and help balance ecology.

Tortoise are very unique animals best known for their slow pace, ability to live very long (ancient historical life) and ability to adopt and live anywhere on earth apart from antarctica. In addition, they are know for the ability to keep breath for a long while among other intriguing features. However, hatching their eggs can be very challenging under normal conditions. They need warm environment where they can breed, a condition that is very rare to find naturally.



Figure 2: A tortoise at DeKUT wildlife conservancy

Environmental sensor development and Kaa IoT Platform
DeKUT wildlife conservancy started developing a tortoise hatchery project and in their preliminary work they designed a tortoise eggs incubator while focusing on their research findings. However, they lacked condition control and monitoring feature to regulate the temperature and humidity which are key conditions for hatching. They are kept at certain level throught otherwise diminishes chances. Temperature for instance determines their gender. Lower temperature produce males while higher temperatures females. Also it is hard to determine their gender when they grow. Normal incubation period is 8 to 11 weeks. However, lower temperatures can lengthen the period. Therefore, approximate hatching temperature is between 26°C to 32°C.

Humidity is also important in hatching process. Tortoise eggs can be soft or hard shelled but most of them are hard shelled hence humidity required by each species may varry. It is hard for one to tell the level of himidity without the use of tools such as hygrometers or calibrated sensors. It should be mantained at about 70%. Low humidity causes pitting and collapsing of the eggs while high humidity causes bacteria growth. We designed and developed a temperature and humidity monitoring sensor node using Internet of Things (IoT) architecture and platform since they had developed the incubator structure.



Figure 3: Initial developed incubator with eggs barried under sawdust

Resources
STM32 Nucleo-64 board F446re (nucleo board), AAM2302B (DHT22) single bus digital temperature and humidity sensor module, ESP8266 Wi-Fi microchip, Jumpers, P56 Casing adaptor box, Arduino Desktop IDE, Kaa IoT free demo account, Micro USB cable and a PC

Prototyping

Hardware Assembling
A Wi-Fi microchip is connected to nucleo board as described in Figure 3. HDT22 sensor temperature sensor is also connected on 3.3V (red) pin, GND (black) and D7 (data). All these modules are connected using female-female jumpers to the board. The circuit is housed in the adaptor box. Power adaptor is connected on Vin and GND of the nucleo board for powering the device during deployment.



Figure 4: Connection of Wi-Fi microchip to nucleo board (source dsa 2017)

Kaa IoT platform Setup and Configuration
To monitor the incubator conditions remotely and help in decision making we connected the device to Kaa IoT platform on free demo account. They allow connection of up to 5 devices among other features offered on free tier. The incubator structure was located in an office with wifi connectivity hence easy to connect the sensor to Kaa IoT platform. We followed Kaa IoT documentation to configure the sensor node on the platform


Figure 5: Kaa IoT platform configuration and device registration

Flashing the program
On the Arduino desktop IDE, relevant libraries and packages were installed. The credentials (endpoint token and application version) obtained from the Kaa IoT platform is set in the code including office Wi-Fi USSID and password. A sketch from this repository and modified from the Kaa IoT documentation is flashed to the nucleo board using micro USB cable.



Figure 6: Flashing temperature and humidity monitoring program to the sensor node

Deployment
The sensor node was deployed in the incubator by mounting it on the inner wall. However, a few changes were made on the incubator structure to achieved required hatching conditions. A 40 Watt bulb was connected in the incubator to control temperature, slightly wet sawdust, small water trough with water and sponge for humidity was placed at one corner of the incubator. Also tortoise eggs were put on a tray and placed in as well. Aeration was adjusted by allowing little ventilation on the walls of the incubator while relying on the sensor measurements on Kaa IoT dashboard untill a stable temperature of approximately 28°C and 75% humidity was achieved. Device power was drawn from power extension nearby and the set up looked as shown in Figure 6.



Figure 7: Sensor deployed in the incubator

Results
On the Kaa IoT dashboard, we customised and set various widgets to visualize temperature and humidity parameters on realtime graphs, The location of the sensor and alarms were set using various colors (yellow, green and red) and a set of limits to trigger email alerts.



Figure 8: Hatched tortoise from the incubator

A dashboard visualizing the data both on pc and mobile phone was successfully set and given to game rangers for use. The dashboard was shared to the rangers by creating a user with read rights. The sensor node has been operational since November 2020 until now. Since then three eggs from have hatched, two survived while one died due to dehydration.

Conclusion and future work
Using IoT environmental sensors enhances optimum hatching conditions. Temperature, humidity, egg conditions and nesting grounds significantly affects hatching process. The developed incubator protects the eggs from predators and harsh environmental conditions. Water and bulb in the incubator enhanced temperature and humidity conditions. Going forward, we intend to include electronic relays for controlling bulb and mini fan to automate the temperature and humidity control process. We intend to design and develop the sensor nodes with callibrated sensors for commercial incubators. In addition, we will develop dedicated tortoise monitoring application for monitoring hatching conditions and alerts.
